# Interview: Linda O'Neil (Director of Sales Enablement)
Date: 2025-10-23
Topic: Training and Ramp Time

**Interviewer:** Linda, thanks for finding a slot. I know you've got a whole new cohort of SDRs starting today. How are they looking?

**Linda:** (Sighs) They're bright-eyed and bushy-tailed, bless them. Ask me again in three weeks when they've had their first 50 rejections. I'm just trying to get them through the "where is the CRM" phase right now.

**Interviewer:** Hah, the onboarding struggle is real. In your experience, what’s the hardest thing to teach a new rep once they actually find the CRM?

**Linda:** Honestly? It’s how to research without getting lost. We have a serious "rabbit hole" syndrome here. A new rep starts researching a company, they click a link to an article, then they click on the author's LinkedIn profile, then they're looking at their college reunion photos from 2014, and suddenly forty minutes have passed and they haven't actually found a reason to call. 

**Interviewer:** "Rabbit hole syndrome," I like it. How do you try to fix that or keep them focused?

**Linda:** We try to give them strict checklists. "Look for X, Y, and Z. Nothing else." 1. Recent funding news—Series B or C preferably. 2. New hiring in engineering or ops. 3. Recent leadership changes at the VP level. That's it. Get in, get out.

**Interviewer:** And where are they primarily finding those X, Y, and Z points?

**Linda:** 9 times out of 10? LinkedIn. Sometimes they'll use Crunchbase or a buried press release on some wire service. But mostly LinkedIn. The problem isn't that the data is missing; it's that they have to sift through so much irrelevant noise to find those three specific things. It’s not just "is the data there?" — it’s "can you find it in under two minutes?" 

**Interviewer:** So the core skill you're teaching is... filtering?

**Linda:** Exactly. We’re essentially teaching them to be high-priced data miners. Which is fine for a while, but it's a terrible use of their talent. If we could just hand them the "signals" on a platter—"Hey, here are the three things you need for this prospect"—we could spend our training time teaching them how to actually *sell*, how to handle objections, how to build rapport, instead of teaching them how to be expert search engine operators. It's a massive drain on our ramp time.
